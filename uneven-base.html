Four classes, four sets of rules: the uneven base beneath the surcharge

Before any new tax on second homes, New York City's property tax already values nearly identical properties by wholly different logics. Here is how the base works — and what that produces — drawn entirely from the City's own data and the governing law.

“Opaque. Arcane. Inequitable.” — New York City's own Advisory Commission on Property Tax Reform, describing the current system, 2021

1 / Every property falls into one of four classes, each taxed differently

State law sorts all New York City property into four classes. Two properties of the very same market value can be assessed at different fractions of that value, and have their value estimated by entirely different methods, depending only on which class they land in.

2 / On top of that, state law caps how fast assessments can rise

Caps were meant to protect homeowners from sudden spikes. But because values rise fastest where homes are most expensive, the caps quietly push the most-appreciated homes to a lower share of their true value over time.

Class 1 — 1–3 family homes

6% per year · 20% over 5 years

Small Class 2 — 2–10 unit co-ops, condos & rentals

8% per year · 30% over 5 years
Finance's own worked example shows the effect: a home whose market value falls from $150,000 to $140,000 can still see its assessed value rise the same year, because the capped figure is catching up from prior years.
3 / What these rules produce, in the City's own numbers

All figures below are from the Department of Finance's data as compiled in the Advisory Commission's 2021 final report. Effective tax rates are expressed per $100 of sales-based market value, using a common valuation method across classes.

25% / 22%
The median co-op carries a Finance value of just 25% of its sale-based market value; the median condo, 22%. The income approach systematically books these homes far below what they sell for. Road to Reform, Tables 4 & 6 (FY2021)
$1.78 → $0.45
Because the gap widens with price, the effective rate on co-ops is regressive: the lowest-valued co-ops pay a median $1.78 per $100 of value, the highest-valued just $0.45. Road to Reform, Table 13 (FY2021)
2.22% vs 4.89%
The caps mean identical-value homes pay different bills. The median Class 1 home is assessed at 2.22% of value in Manhattan but 4.89% on Staten Island — against a 6% target. Road to Reform, Table 9 (FY2021)
48% / 14%
The classes shoulder the levy unevenly. Class 1 holds 48% of the City's assessed value but pays 14% of the tax; Class 4 holds 24% of value and pays 42%. Road to Reform, Table 10 (FY2021)

Where it stands legally

In March 2024 the New York Court of Appeals revived Tax Equity Now NY LLC v. City of New York, allowing claims to proceed that the system imposes unequal bills on similarly valued properties — in violation of state Real Property Tax Law § 305(2) and the federal Fair Housing Act, on which the suit alleges a discriminatory disparate impact.
